Retail Superstore EDA

Exploratory data analysis on a retail sales dataset, digging into what actually drives revenue across categories, regions, and time.

2025 · Individual project · Jupyter Notebook
Add cover image at
assets/img/projects/eda-cover.jpg

Before building models, I wanted to get genuinely fluent in exploratory data analysis — the step where you actually understand a dataset instead of rushing to model it. I used a retail Superstore sales dataset to practice that discipline end to end.

Python Pandas Matplotlib Seaborn

How I built it

Working in a Jupyter notebook, I started with the basics — checking for nulls, duplicate rows, and inconsistent category labels — before moving into analysis. I used pandas to group and aggregate sales by category, sub-category, region, and time period, then used Matplotlib and Seaborn to visualize the patterns that emerged.

A few patterns stood out: sales were concentrated in a handful of sub-categories, certain regions consistently outperformed others even after accounting for order volume, and there were clear seasonal spikes worth flagging for anyone using this data to plan inventory or promotions.

Challenges & what I learned

The biggest lesson wasn't technical — it was resisting the urge to jump straight to charts before understanding what each column actually meant and how clean the data really was. A couple of early visualizations were misleading until I traced them back to duplicate rows I hadn't caught yet.

What I learned

Good EDA is mostly disciplined skepticism: checking your own numbers before trusting them, and asking whether a pattern is real or an artifact of how the data was collected. That habit has carried directly into how I approach every dataset since, including the ones behind my ML projects.
