AI-Powered WhatsApp Finance Assistant

A conversational assistant that helps people track spending and manage their money right inside WhatsApp — built end-to-end with a teammate as our AIGC600 capstone.

2026 · Team project · Capstone, AI Integration & Governance

Personal finance apps ask people to change their habits and open a new app. We wanted something that met people where they already are — so we built a finance assistant that lives inside WhatsApp, where you can log an expense, ask about your spending, and get a nudge back in plain conversation.

n8n WhatsApp Business API Webhooks Automation

How I built it

The assistant runs on n8n as the automation and orchestration layer. When a user sends a WhatsApp message, a webhook triggers a workflow that parses the message, routes it to the right logic branch (logging an expense, answering a spending question, or setting a reminder), and sends a reply back through the WhatsApp Business API.

I worked with a teammate to split the build: I focused on the webhook setup, message-routing logic, and closing a couple of issues that came up during testing — including a hardcoded API token that was sitting in plain text in one of our workflow nodes, which I moved into n8n's credential store, and a run of WhatsApp webhook verification errors that turned out to be a mismatch between our callback URL and the verify token Meta expected.

Challenges & what I learned

The security issue was the one that stuck with me most. It's easy to hardcode a token to get something working fast, and just as easy to forget it's there. Fixing it meant going back through every node that touched the API and moving credentials into n8n's built-in credential manager instead — a small change that completely changed how safe the workflow was to share or hand off.

What I learned

Debugging the webhook verification errors taught me to read platform documentation line by line instead of assuming I understood the handshake — Meta's verification step is strict about exact string matches, and the fix was smaller than the hours I spent finding it. It reinforced that security and integration details deserve the same care as the "interesting" logic.

Turning this into a Medium post afterward also pushed me to explain the project clearly to people outside the course — a good test of whether I actually understood what I'd built, not just that it worked.
